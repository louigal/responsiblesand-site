Why join

Sand and silicates are in almost everything we build, make and use. Almost no one is working on how to source them responsibly. Not yet.

This is early. That's the point.

The data on sand and silicates is thin. The standards don't cover them yet. The risks are real — but so are the opportunities, for the companies, researchers and policymakers who move now.

The Coalition exists to do this work together, before the field hardens around assumptions rather than evidence.

A head start on the evidence

The Coalition's work builds on two foundational studies — one for the OECD, one for GIZ — and members get access to the findings, the analysis and the conversations around them as they develop. In a topic where good data is scarce, that's a real advantage.

A seat at the table while norms are forming

Sand and silicates will come onto responsible-minerals agendas. The question is whether the standards and expectations that emerge reflect how things actually work, or whether they're written by people who don't know. Members help make sure it's the former.

Work that produces something real

This isn't a talking group. Members contribute experience, test ideas in real supply chains, and help turn learning into guidance that others can use. The emphasis is on shared problem-solving, not positioning.

Sand and silicates are everywhere

From the glass in semiconductors to the aggregate in concrete, from the kaolin in ceramics to the sand in solar panels — these materials touch almost every supply chain. That's why this work has to be cross-sector.

Grounded in research

The Coalition's initial work builds on two foundational studies led by the University of Queensland, 2024–2026:

A study for the OECD on due diligence for sand and silicates supply chains, publishing in early 2026. It confirms these materials belong on international responsible-minerals agendas and documents major gaps in existing standards, tools and practice.

A study for GIZ on raw materials in semiconductor supply chains, currently underway. It situates sand and silicates within wider debates on supply security, due diligence and industrial policy.

Together, these form a shared analytical baseline — the starting point for the guidance, tools and demonstrations developed with members. This work also builds on earlier research the UQ team undertook with the United Nations Environment Programme.

What we're working on

Over the first three years, members can expect the Coalition to:

  1. Define what responsible sand and silicates means in practice, and turn it into usable guidance.
  2. Test due-diligence and risk tools in selected supply chains.
  3. Deliver one or more demonstration projects in live settings.
  4. Produce accessible guidance, case material and communications.
  5. Keep the conversation going — sharpening results and finding new opportunities as we learn.

Built for you

For industry

Whether you're in construction, glass, technology or manufacturing, the Coalition gives you early visibility of where expectations are heading and a direct hand in shaping them. You contribute what you know about how things actually work. You get back guidance that reflects operational reality.

For researchers and civil society

If you work on resource governance, biodiversity, social performance or circular economy, the Coalition is a place to connect that expertise to real supply chains. Test ideas. Influence practice. Help build something that lasts.

For policymakers and foundations

The Coalition offers a way to co-design research and action agendas with impact built in from the start. Connect policy to practice. Translate foresight into workable approaches for materials governance.
